Early 2025, a funny thing happened: hundreds of thousands of "TikTok refugees" downloaded Xiaohongshu (we call it RedNote in English) and got their first real peek at what Chinese social media actually looks like. The clips were hilarious—Americans asking how to recycle in Shanghai, locals patiently explaining that yes, tap water is safe in most cities, and no, you can't bring your dog to brunch like you would at home.

It was the kind of cross-cultural moment a lot of us who've spent years studying China have waited for. But here's the problem: when those same TikTok refugees started asking the obvious follow-ups—_Where do I learn Mandarin?_ _What C-drama should I watch first?_ _How do I actually use WeChat?_—there was no good answer.

Oh, there were answers. Always answers. A grad student would point you to the [Chinese Grammar Wiki](/category/learn-chinese). A foodie on Reddit would link [The Woks of Life](/category/food). A film nerd would mention Letterboxd. But nowhere on the open internet was there a single page that said: _Here are the 60 things we'd actually hand a curious friend._

So I built one. This is part explanation, part confession of what I got wrong along the way, and mostly a map for anyone else who finds themselves suddenly curious about China and has no idea where to start.

## What I thought I was building (and why I was wrong)

At first, I wanted this site to be **The Wirecutter for China**. Definitive picks, no fluff—"If you only have time for one thing, here it is."

That idea crashed and burned almost immediately. Two reasons:

First, "definitive" is a trap. The best podcast on Chinese history (in my book, it's [The China History Podcast](/category/culture)) has 400+ episodes. It's genuinely amazing—_and_ totally wrong for someone who just learned the word "dynasty" and has 30 minutes on their Tuesday commute. Good curation needs to balance quality _and_ fit. Pretending you can cram both into a single "best" list is how directories die.

Second, the stuff that actually helps you "get" China isn't on a list. It's a YouTube comment thread where someone explains _why_ a certain dish matters. It's the moment your Cantonese coworker laughs at how you pronounce _xièxie_. It's Xiaohongshu's algorithm showing you minimalist Hangzhou apartments until suddenly something clicks. A directory can point to doors, but it can't walk you through them. The "ULTIMATE GUIDE TO CHINESE CULTURE" posts? They read like what they are: SEO bait.

So I started over. New premise: **11 doorways, each annotated by someone who's actually spent time inside.** Sixty-some entries, each one a little piece of my own taste rather than a ranking.

## The 11 doorways

If you're new here, here's what's behind each door:

- **[Learn Chinese](/category/learn-chinese)** — Apps, dictionaries, podcasts, grammar references. This is where I'm most opinionated: Start with [HelloChinese](https://www.hellochinese.cc/) for tones (they'll make or break your Mandarin), install [Pleco](https://www.pleco.com/) the day you decide you're serious, and read [Hacking Chinese](https://www.hackingchinese.com/) when you hit that week-three wall and wonder if you're doing this right.

- **[Culture & History](/category/culture)** — The Met's Asian art collection is online. The Palace Museum's archive is online. [Asianometry](https://www.youtube.com/@Asianometry) makes long video essays about Chinese industrial history that are basically HBO-quality. None of this is hard to find—you just need to know where to look.

- **[Food & Cuisine](/category/food)** — The densest category by far, because Chinese food online is having a _moment_. [Chinese Cooking Demystified](https://www.youtube.com/@ChineseCookingDemystified) and [Made With Lau](https://www.madewithlau.com/) are the YouTube channels that finally taught foreigners what we should've known all along: "Chinese food" isn't one cuisine—it's eight, pretending to be one for Western menus.

- **[Travel](/category/travel)** — As of 2024, most Westerners can now spend 10 days in Shanghai without a visa. Most Americans have no idea. This whole category exists to fix that.

- **[Living in China](/category/living)** — For the smaller crowd actually moving there. Visas, banking, the WeChat-Alipay duopoly, healthcare. It's drier than the other sections, but this is where outdated info can get you in real trouble.

- **[Apps & Platforms](/category/apps)** — The Chinese internet runs on 6 or 7 super-apps, and almost no one outside China can name three. Start with WeChat (you'll need it), Xiaohongshu (you'll probably like it), and Bilibili (you'll fall down the rabbit hole).

- **[Entertainment](/category/entertainment)** — C-dramas, films, donghua. Viki and iQIYI are the easiest legal ways in. If you only watch one C-drama? It depends on who you are—but _The Long Season_ (2023) is the safe bet. It's just a great show that happens to be set in northeast China.

- **[Business & Tech](/category/business)** — BYD, DJI, DeepSeek, Xiaomi. These four tell you most of what you need to know about Chinese hardware and AI in the late 2020s. I made this category because the financial press treats them all like geopolitical headlines first and companies second. Exhausting.

- **[Media & News](/category/media)** — The hardest one to curate, because "good China coverage" is political by definition. My honest advice: Read three sources at once, triangulate, and distrust anyone—foreign _or_ Chinese—who never changes their mind.

- **[Diaspora & Chinatowns](/category/diaspora)** — Usually missing from "learn about China" lists, which is crazy. About 50 million people of Chinese descent live outside the PRC—they're often the first Chinese people you'll actually meet. Check out MOCA in New York, Chinatown Rising in SF, and 88rising for the music.

- **[Subcultures & Trends](/category/subculture)** — Hanfu revival, guochao (national-trend) fashion, citywalk, the ACG scene. This category will age the fastest. That's okay—it's where the real cultural energy is. A directory that only covered Tang poetry would be lying about what young China is actually doing in 2026.

## What I left out

For transparency, here's what I considered including but cut:

**Anything behind a hard paywall.** There's great China analysis in $500/year newsletters. Recommending those to someone just starting out? Rude.

**News outlets I read daily but can't fairly recommend.** Some of the best China commentary comes from individual analysts on Substack and Twitter that I trust deeply—_because I've followed them for years_. I can't hand those to a stranger and call them "balanced." They're important to _my_ media diet, but that's different from a directory entry.

**Personality-driven creators mixed with institutions.** It felt wrong putting one person's YouTube channel next to a museum collection. They're not the same thing. So the directory only lists sites and institutions for now—I'm working on a dedicated Creators section.

**Anything I couldn't access from California without a VPN.** This hurt. Some of the most interesting writing about contemporary China is on Chinese platforms with no stable English mirrors. They get a note ("requires VPN outside China") but not a featured spot. Curiosity shouldn't require a $5/month subscription as step one.

## How it's actually built

For the nerds: It's Astro, Tailwind, and a folder of markdown files in a public GitHub repo. Every resource is a `.md` file with frontmatter. Search runs client-side via Pagefind—no database, no backend. Hosted on Cloudflare Pages for basically free.

The repo is at [github.com/pkloving/china-discovery-hub](https://github.com/pkloving/china-discovery-hub). If you know a resource I'm missing—and I definitely am—use the [submit form](/submit) or open an issue. This is a personal editorial project, so PRs aren't merged directly; every suggestion goes through me first.

## What I hope this becomes

Right now: A directory.

In six months: A directory plus a small library of essays like this—one per category, written by someone who actually uses those resources, not a content marketer chasing "best Chinese learning apps 2026."

In a year, if people care: Maybe a quiet newsletter, the Creators section, possibly Spanish (it's the next biggest language for China curiosity). No accounts, no comments, no growth hacking.

Here's the thing that keeps me working on this: **The internet was supposed to be good at this.** Cross-cultural curiosity is what the web does best—when it's allowed to. Algorithms shrank the surface area. Directories like this are a small bet that hand-curation, plus fixing broken links, plus a public repo, can do something algorithms can't.
