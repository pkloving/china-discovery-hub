In early 2025, a few hundred thousand "TikTok refugees" landed on Xiaohongshu (RedNote) and discovered, for the first time, what the Chinese internet actually looks like from the inside. The clips that came out of those weeks were genuinely funny — Americans asking how to recycle bottles in Shanghai, Chinese users politely explaining that yes, you can drink the tap water in some cities, no, you can't bring your dog to brunch the way you do at home.

It was the kind of cross-cultural moment a generation of China watchers had been quietly waiting for. And yet, when those same TikTok refugees started asking the obvious follow-up questions — *where do I learn Mandarin?* *what's a good C-drama to start with?* *how do I actually use WeChat?* — there was no good answer.

There were answers, plural. There were always answers. A grad student would point them at the [Chinese Grammar Wiki](/category/learn-chinese). A foodie on Reddit would link [The Woks of Life](/category/food). A film nerd would mention Letterboxd. But there was no single page on the open internet that said: *here are the sixty things we'd hand a curious friend.*

So I made one. This essay is partly an explanation of what I built, partly a confession of what I got wrong on the way, and mostly a map for anyone else who finds themselves suddenly interested in China and not sure where to start.

## What I thought I was building (and was wrong about)

The first version of this site, in my head, was going to be **The Wirecutter for China**. Definitive picks per category, no fluff, "if you only have time for one thing, here it is."

That fell apart almost immediately. Two reasons:

**The first**: "definitive" is the wrong frame. The single best podcast on Chinese history (in my opinion: [The China History Podcast](/category/culture)) is also a 400-episode behemoth. It is genuinely the best, *and* it's the wrong recommendation for someone who has thirty minutes on a Tuesday commute and just learned the word "dynasty." Curation has to do two jobs at once — quality *and* fit — and pretending you can compress those into one ranking is how directories die.

**The second**: a lot of what makes someone "get" China is not on a list of products. It's a YouTube comment thread. It's the moment your Cantonese coworker laughs at how you pronounce *xièxie*. It's the way Xiaohongshu's algorithm keeps showing you minimalist apartment tours in Hangzhou until something clicks. A directory can point at doors, but it can't walk you through them, and the entries that pretend otherwise (the "ULTIMATE GUIDE TO CHINESE CULTURE" types) read as exactly what they are: SEO chum.

So I rebuilt around a different premise: **eleven doorways, each annotated by someone who's spent time inside.** Sixty-some entries, each one a small confession of taste rather than a vote in a ranking.

## The eleven doors

If you came to this site cold, here's what's behind each:

- **[Learn Chinese](/category/learn-chinese)** — Apps, dictionaries, podcasts, grammar references. The single category where my personal recommendation is the most opinionated: start with [HelloChinese](https://www.hellochinese.cc/) for tones, install [Pleco](https://www.pleco.com/) the day you commit, and read [Hacking Chinese](https://www.hackingchinese.com/) when you start feeling lost about *how* to study (which will be week three).
- **[Culture & History](/category/culture)** — The Met's Asian art collection is online. The Palace Museum's online archive is online. [Asianometry](https://www.youtube.com/@Asianometry) makes long video essays about Chinese industrial history that are good enough to be HBO shows. None of this is hard to find once you know to look.
- **[Food & Cuisine](/category/food)** — Probably the densest single category, because Chinese food online is having a *moment*. [Chinese Cooking Demystified](https://www.youtube.com/@ChineseCookingDemystified) and [Made With Lau](https://www.madewithlau.com/) are the YouTube channels that finally taught a generation of foreigners that "Chinese food" isn't a cuisine — it's eight cuisines pretending to be one for the convenience of Western menus.
- **[Travel](/category/travel)** — As of 2024, the visa-free transit list quietly grew to cover most of the West. Most Americans don't know they can spend ten days in Shanghai without a visa now. This whole category exists to fix that gap.
- **[Living in China](/category/living)** — For the much smaller crowd actually moving. Visas, banking, the WeChat-Alipay duopoly, healthcare. Drier than the other categories. Also the one where outdated information will get you in real trouble.
- **[Apps & Platforms](/category/apps)** — The Chinese internet runs on six or seven super-apps and almost no one outside China can name three of them. Start with WeChat (you'll need it), Xiaohongshu (you'll like it), and Bilibili (you'll fall in).
- **[Entertainment](/category/entertainment)** — C-dramas, films, donghua. Viki and iQIYI are the easiest legal entry points. If you only watch one C-drama: a different recommendation for everyone, but the safe one for foreigners is *The Long Season* (2023), because it is just a great show and happens to be set in northeast China.
- **[Business & Tech](/category/business)** — BYD, DJI, DeepSeek, Xiaomi. These four companies tell you most of what you need to know about where Chinese hardware and AI are in the late 2020s. The category exists because the financial press treats every one of them as a geopolitical headline first and a company second, which is exhausting.
- **[Media & News](/category/media)** — The hardest category to curate, because what counts as "good China coverage" is itself politically loaded. The honest version: read three at once, triangulate, distrust anyone (foreign *or* Chinese) who never updates their priors.
- **[Diaspora & Chinatowns](/category/diaspora)** — Often missing from "learn about China" lists, which is silly given that about 50 million people of Chinese descent live outside the PRC and many of them are the people you'll actually meet first. MOCA in New York, Chinatown Rising in San Francisco, 88rising for the music.
- **[Subcultures & Trends](/category/subculture)** — Hanfu revival, guochao (national-trend) fashion, citywalk, the ACG scene. This category will age the fastest. That's fine; it's also where most of the actual cultural energy is, and a directory that only covered Tang poetry would be lying about what young China is doing in 2026.

## What I cut

For honesty: things I considered including and chose not to.

**Anything behind a hard paywall with no preview.** Plenty of excellent China analysis lives behind $500/year newsletters. Recommending those to a curious newcomer would be obnoxious.

**News outlets I personally consume daily but can't fairly recommend.** Some of the best China commentary on the English internet comes from individual analysts on Substack and Twitter whose work I deeply trust *because I've followed them for years*. I can't honestly hand those to a stranger as "balanced." They're not — they're load-bearing in *my* media diet, and that's different from a directory entry.

**Personality-driven creators in the same lists as institutions.** It feels wrong to put one person's YouTube channel next to a museum collection — they're not the same kind of thing, even if both are excellent. So the directory currently only lists sites and institutions; a dedicated *Creators* section is in the queue.

**Anything I couldn't reach from California without a VPN.** This was the painful one. Some of the most interesting writing about contemporary Chinese culture is on Chinese-language platforms that don't have stable English-accessible mirrors. They get a line in the description ("requires VPN from outside China") rather than a featured slot. Foreign curiosity should not require a $5/month VPN subscription as the first step.

## How it's actually built

For anyone curious about the boring parts: it's Astro, Tailwind, and a folder of markdown files in a public GitHub repo. Every resource is a `.md` file with frontmatter. Search runs entirely client-side via Pagefind — no database, no backend. Hosted on Cloudflare Pages for around $0/year.

The repo is at [github.com/pkloving/china-discovery-hub](https://github.com/pkloving/china-discovery-hub). If you know a resource I'm missing — and I am definitely missing some — use the [submit form](/submit) or open an Issue on the repo. The site is a personal editorial project, so PRs aren't merged; every suggestion goes through one set of editorial hands and either gets added or politely declined.

## What I'd like this to grow into

Right now: a directory.

In six months: a directory plus a small library of essays like this one — one per category, in the voice of someone who actually uses the resources in that category, not a content marketer optimizing for "best Chinese learning apps 2026."

In a year, if there's an audience: probably a quiet newsletter, maybe a Creators section, possibly a second language (Spanish is the next-largest non-English curiosity I'd want to serve well). Nothing involving accounts, comments, or growth-hacking.

The whole project is shaped by a small belief I haven't been able to shake: **the internet was supposed to be good at this**. Cross-cultural curiosity is one of the things the web does best — when it's allowed to. Algorithms collapsed the surface area; directories like this are a small bet that hand-curation, plus link rot maintenance, plus a public GitHub repo, can do something the algorithms can't.

If you read this far: thanks. [Start exploring](/#categories), or [tell me what's missing](/submit).
